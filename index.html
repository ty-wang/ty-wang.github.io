Research Projects
Directional Compliance in Obstacle-Aided Navigation for Snake Robots
(Accepeted) American Control Conference (ACC), 2020   (Oral Presentation)
pdf / video

Snake robots have the potential to maneuver through tightly packed and complex environments. One challenge in enabling them to do so is the complexity in determining how to coordinate their many degrees-of-freedom to create purposeful motion. This is especially true in the types of terrains considered in this work: environments full of unmodeled features that even the best of maps would not capture, motivating us to develop closed-loop controls to react to those features. To accomplish this, this work uses proprioceptive sensing, mainly the force information measured by the snake robotâ€™s joints, to react to unmodeled terrain. We introduce a biologically-inspired strategy called directional compliance which modulates the effective stiffness of the robot so that it conforms to the terrain in some directions and resists in others. We present a dynamical system that switches between modes of locomotion to handle situations in which the robot gets wedged or stuck. This approach enables the snake robot to reliably traverse a planar peg array and an outdoor three-dimensional pile of rocks.
Snake Robot 3D Compliant Shape Control and Motion Planning
Ongoing project

Mimicking biological snakes, snake robots undulate their bodies to generate movement. To move in three-dimensional environments, the robot needs to employ sinusoidal undulations in both lateral and dorsal planes. However, due to the non-commutativity of rotations, the robot cannot form the desired configurations if its joint angles are directly assigned by theoretical rotation angles. This work uses optimization tools to solve the non-commutativity problem, enabling snake robots to conform to and exploit three-dimensional obstacle features in unknown and unmodeled environments.
Biologically Inspired Snake Robot Turn-in-Place Control
Ongoing project

Inspired by the movement of a species of worms, Caenorhabditis elegans, this work develops a novel motion pattern of the snake robot which allows the robot to turn in place with high speed in granular medium, on hard ground, or between obstacles.
